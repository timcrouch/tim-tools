Identifying Metrics That Matter

The Measurement Challenge

AI productivity gains are real but often difficult to quantify precisely because:

  • Benefits are distributed across many small tasks
  • Baseline measurements often don't exist
  • Quality improvements are subjective
  • Time savings don't always translate to output increases

Categories of AI Impact

  • Time-Based: Hours saved per task type, faster turnaround
  • Output-Based: More work completed, more proposals submitted
  • Quality-Based: Fewer revisions, reduced errors, better consistency
  • Business Impact: Revenue per employee, project profitability, win rates

The Metrics Hierarchy

Level Example Metrics Value
Activity (avoid)Queries made, time in toolsLow insight
EfficiencyTime saved per task, turnaround timeModerate insight

üí° Focus on Level 2 and 3

Activity metrics tell you adoption is happening but not whether it's valuable.

Tracking Productivity Gains Realistically

Establishing Baselines

Method 1: Historical Analysis

Review past project timesheets, analyze pre-AI completion times, document typical revision cycles.

Limitation: Past data may not exist or be comparable.

Method 2: Before/After Snapshots

Measure key metrics before AI rollout, re-measure after adoption stabilizes (2-3 months).

Limitation: Other factors may change simultaneously.

Method 3: The Time Diary Method

  1. Select 10-15 representative tasks
  2. Have team members log time for these tasks
  3. Track over 2-4 weeks pre-AI
  4. Track same tasks post-AI adoption
  5. Calculate average time differences

Avoiding Vanity Metrics

What Are Vanity Metrics?

Metrics that look impressive but don't indicate actual value creation.

‚ùå Vanity Metrics

  • Number of AI licenses purchased
  • Total AI queries made
  • Hours "spent with AI"
  • AI tool login frequency

‚úÖ Actionable Metrics

  • Time saved on specific task types
  • Increase in deliverables produced
  • Reduction in revision cycles
  • Client satisfaction changes
  • Revenue per employee trends

Questions to Test Metric Quality

  1. Does this metric connect to business outcomes?
  2. Would gaming this metric be harmful?
  3. Does this metric help us make decisions?
  4. Can we act on this metric?

Building the Business Case for Expanded AI Investment

ROI Calculation Models

Simple Payback Model

Example:

  • Investment: $50,000/year (25 licenses √ó $2,000/year)
  • Time Savings: 5 hours/user/week √ó 25 users = 125 hours/week
  • Value of Time: 125 hours √ó $100/hour = $12,500/week
  • Annual Savings: $12,500 √ó 50 weeks = $625,000
  • Payback Period: ~1 month

Conservative ROI Model

Apply reality factors:

  • Utilization Factor: 60-70% (not everyone uses tools fully)
  • Productivity Capture: 50-70% (time saved doesn't always become productive)
  • Ramp Time: 6 months to full productivity

Frame the Investment Correctly

‚ùå Weak Framing

"We need AI tools because everyone else has them."

‚úÖ Strong Framing

"A $50,000 AI investment will increase our team's capacity by 25%, enabling us to take on $200,000 more revenue without adding headcount."

Key Messages for Executives

  1. Competitive necessity: "Our competitors are achieving faster turnarounds using AI."
  2. Capacity without headcount: "AI lets us scale output without proportionally scaling costs."
  3. Quality consistency: "AI helps maintain quality standards as we grow."
  4. Talent attraction: "Top candidates expect AI tools."

When AI Delivers Real Value vs. Hype

Genuine Value Indicators ‚úÖ

  • Measurable time savings on specific tasks
  • Increased output with same headcount
  • Quality maintained or improved
  • Sustainable behavior change (people keep using it)
  • Competitive advantage achieved
  • Practitioners report genuine benefit

Red Flags for AI Hype ‚ö†Ô∏è

  • Benefits are vague ("AI makes us more innovative")
  • Metrics focus on activity, not outcomes
  • Claims are absolute ("AI will revolutionize everything")
  • Costs are unclear or hidden
  • Use cases are forced
  • Adoption requires constant pushing

Reality-Check Questions

  1. Would we pay for this if it weren't "AI"?
  2. What would happen if we removed this tool tomorrow?
  3. Are our best performers using this?
  4. Has anything actually changed?
  5. Can we attribute specific outcomes to AI?

üèãÔ∏è Hands-On Exercise: ROI Calculation

Duration: 25 minutes

Part 1: Identify Your Use Case (5 min)

Select one specific AI use case from your organization (a task frequently performed).

Part 2: Estimate Current State (5 min)

  • How many times per month is this task performed?
  • How many people perform this task?
  • Average time per task (current)?
  • Loaded labor cost per hour?

Part 3: Estimate AI-Assisted State (5 min)

  • Estimated time per task with AI?
  • Time savings per task?
  • Monthly hours saved?
  • Annual value of time saved?

Part 4: Calculate ROI (5 min)

  • Annual AI tool cost for this use case
  • Annual value created
  • Net annual benefit
  • ROI percentage
  • Payback period

Part 5: Reality Check (5 min)

Apply conservative adjustments:

  • Utilization factor (60-70%)
  • Productivity capture (50-70%)
  • Is this investment justified?

Key Takeaways

  1. Measure outcomes, not activity ‚Äî Queries and logins tell you nothing about value; time saved and output increased do
  2. Establish baselines before measuring ‚Äî You can't show improvement without knowing where you started
  3. Account for the learning curve ‚Äî Don't judge ROI too early; wait 2-3 months for meaningful data
  4. Use conservative assumptions ‚Äî Not all time saved becomes productive output; build in reality factors
  5. Build multi-dimensional metrics ‚Äî Balance efficiency, quality, output, and business impact measures
  6. Separate hype from value ‚Äî Real value shows up in specific, measurable outcomes, not vague claims
  7. Make the business case concrete ‚Äî Executives respond to clear ROI calculations, not enthusiasm
